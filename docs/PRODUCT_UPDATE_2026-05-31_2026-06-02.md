## 1. Dev.to version

# What if duo MMD scenes and vertical Shorts just worked—in your browser?

**AnimaStage Lite** is a browser-native MikuMikuDance studio: load models and motion, preview with physics, and export video—no install, no Windows-only toolchain. Over three intense days (31 May – 2 Jun), we closed the gap between “cool tech demo” and something you can actually ship.

Everything runs **client-side** (WebGL + WASM physics). No render farm. No desktop app required.

---

## Multi-character scenes

**Problem:** Most web MMD tools feel built for one dancer. Duets mean painful workarounds—or giving up.

**Solution:** You can now drop a folder or ZIP with **up to four PMX/PMD models** in one go. Motions distribute across characters automatically, and the scene places them in sensible stage positions for solo or duo.

**Result:** Build **two-character scenes** the way desktop MMD users expect—drag, play, adjust—still entirely in the tab.

---

## See if your machine can keep up

**Problem:** Heavy models choke the GPU, and you only find out after everything loads.

**Solution:** A live **FPS and triangle counter** in the viewport shows how hard the scene is pushing your GPU.

**Result:** You can judge performance **before** you commit to a long export or add another character—especially on laptops and integrated graphics.

---

## A real workflow—not just a viewport

**Problem:** Great viewers exist; fewer tools cover save, share, templates, and “come back tomorrow.”

**Solution:** A full **product layer** on top of the engine: project save/load, shareable viewer links, scene templates (~50s rolls), beginner mode, and onboarding that doesn’t shove you into expert panels on day one.

**Result:** Open → try a demo → save progress → share a link → open it elsewhere or **fork back into the editor**. That’s a workflow, not a one-off session.

---

## Shorts that don’t murder your dance

### Breakthrough #1

**Problem:** “Generate Short” used to feel like a trap: vertical format came on, but your **original VMD dance could reset or get replaced** by procedural templates. Creators lost the motion they cared about.

**Solution:** Shorts now use a **dedicated path** that keeps your character motion intact, switches to 9:16, tunes FX for performance, and lets you set **length (20–90 seconds)** and **which motion file** each character uses—plus add new VMDs without re-importing the whole model.

**Result:** **Vertical Shorts/Reels-style output that still dances the way you loaded it.** Pick duration, pick motion, preview, export.

---

## Camera that follows the performance—not a frozen hand

### Breakthrough #2

**Problem:** Cinematic camera templates (orbit + bloom) sounded great until the frame locked on a **hand or empty space** while the model moved away. Exports looked broken, not cinematic.

**Solution:** Camera motion now **tracks where the characters actually are**—solo or duo—during preview and export. Orbit paths move with the stage, not a fixed point in the void. In director mode you can also flip **Manual** and place the camera yourself, then go back to auto-framing.

**Result:** **Emote and dance templates feel like a flying camera**, not a stuck security cam. Exports keep the subject in frame for 9:16.

---

## Export you can finish

**Problem:** Long MP4 exports could die mid-run with obscure codec errors, or stutter because frames weren’t handled cleanly.

**Solution:** More resilient **browser-native video encoding** (WebCodecs): better recovery when the browser reclaims the encoder, cleaner frame lifecycle, steadier capture before each frame is written.

**Result:** **Longer vertical exports are more likely to complete** on Chrome/Edge—still 100% client-side, still no upload to a server for rendering.

---

## Stability you don’t see—but feel

Crashes from runaway UI updates, post-effects initializing too early, and landing-page redirects that skipped the homepage—all addressed. Templates now span the **full timeline length** you expect (~50s), not a truncated slice.

---

This isn’t positioning as a tech flex or a one-scene demo.

It’s a **browser-based animation workflow**: multi-character import → directorial camera → vertical export → share → edit again—all without installing MMD.
